Understanding Patents For Your Business

What is a Patent?

A patent is a legal instrument issued by a government that confers to the inventor of a product the sole right to exploit it, that is manufacture, use, sell or dispose of the product for a limited time of 20 years. This means that for this period of time no other person or firm can legally make use or even sell the invention in question without the consent of the patent holder. Patents can be sought for all manner of inventions, including new technologies and methods, products and materials, design, and chemicals.

The heart of a patent is said to be in its barring of others from generating revenue from the invention in question. It makes inventors disclose their inventions to the public to receive a temporary exclusive right over the invention. After a certain time has elapsed, normally the period of validity of the patent, the invention becomes free for anyone to use.

What is Patent Opposition?

Patent opposition is in essence a legal process whereby third parties are allowed to neutralize the grant of a particular patent or a patent application. This mechanism is very important in a way that only meritorious inventions which have the potential to be unique in the market will be granted protection by the patent system. Patent opposition is a legal proceeding where if a person feels that a patent ought not to have been granted â say because the invention is unoriginal, obvious or does not have a practical use in industry â they can file an opposition

The following are some of the reasons that Patent Opposition is very important:

Opposition is a crucial part of ensuring that there is credibility regarding the matters of patents. Hereâs why it matters:

Prevents Unjust Patents: It assists in preventing the grant of patents on inventions that do not satisfy the legal requirements, nature, quality or kind such as novelty or non-obviousness.

Protects Public Interest: In so doing, it opens an opportunity for third parties to oppose what they consider to be unjustified patents in order that the general public is not unduly fettered.

Encourages Fair Competition: For it protects individual companies from monopolizing markets through patents that ought not to have been issued in the first place, it encourages competition.

Types of Patent Opposition

There are two main types of patent opposition: There are two main types of patent opposition:

  • 1. Pre-Grant Opposition

Opposition may occur before the grant of the patent where it takes place after the publication of the application but before the patent is issued. It also permits any third party to object to the application on a variety of pre-scribed grounds such as novelty or obviousness. This process assists in the elimination of applications that should not go to grant.

  • 2. Post-Grant Opposition

Opposition takes place after a grant of the patent has been awarded but before the grant of a certificate of infringement or registration of the patent. Every year any interested person may appeal against the grant of a patent in the belief that it was wrongly granted. If so it results in the cancellation or alteration of the patent.

  • 2. Submitting Evidence:

This one is presented with details from both sides; the opponent and the patent holder produce evidence. This finally comes with the opponent substantiating evidence on why the patent should not be issued, while the holder of the patent responds to the points of the opponent.

  • 3. Hearing:

Inter-party hearings may be conducted where the two parties or either of them may call on the patent office to advance arguments. This is also a chance to explain certain matters and answer the questions, the examiner might have.

  • 4. Decision:

Finally, the patent office makes a verdict after going through the proofs and the arguments and presenting them. Based on the realizations the patent may be sustained, altered, or cancelled.

What is Patent Litigation?

Litigation involves the legal procedures that follow, once one party feels that the other is violating their patent rights. This usually entails a patent infringement case under which the owner of the patent takes the other party (the infringer) to court for using, creating or distributing an invention or goods under the patent without the approval of the owner.Â 

Litigation plays the most significant function in the enforcement of the patent and making sure that the persons who own these patents enjoy their monopoly on inventions for a particular period.

The Patent Litigation Process

  1. Filing the Complaint:

The procedure starts under the assumption of the patentee who files a patent infringement suit in a court of law. The complaint provides information on the patented invention, the infringement of the patent, and the prayer which can be in the form of damages and or an injunction restraining the defendant from continued infringement of the patent.

  1. Discovery:

Discovery is the process by which both parties to the case assemble supporting evidence for the caseâs allegations. This phase is usually characterized by the interchange of documents, deposition and assessment of a patent as well as the derivate product or service that is under scrutiny.

  1. Pre-Trial Motions:

During the pre-trial stage, the parties may CC some motions in the trial to endeavour to solve certain problems or even have the case thrown out. Other motions are those in support of a motion for a summary judgment whereby a party will pray for the matter to be decided without any trial on the basis of the facts.

  1. Trial:

In the case of trial, the lawyers of both parties make their arguments and bring in their evidence and witnesses including the experts. The court then goes ahead to decide if the defendant has indeed infringed on the patent rights of the plaintiff and if so, the court is then called upon to determine which remedy is to be provided.

  1. Post-Trial Motions and Appeals:

Post-trial after the rendition of a verdict, a motion can be made to vacate the verdict or change either the judgment or verdict. If the motions are denied, the party that was adverse to it can move to the appellate level to seek a review.
